Marcus teaches middle school science and runs three separate side activities: after-school tutoring ($4,200 net this year), weekend event photography ($3,100 net), and occasionally reselling old classroom supplies and used camera gear on eBay ($320 net). He's always reported his tutoring and photography income carefully — those feel like "real" side businesses. The eBay sales felt small enough that he wasn't sure they mattered much for taxes.

They do — not because $320 alone crosses any meaningful threshold, but because of how self-employment income combines once you have more than one source.

## Why the eBay Income Isn't as Separate as It Feels

Marcus's instinct that $320 alone seems too small to worry about isn't wrong on its own — $320 is under the $400 self-employment tax threshold if it stood alone. But the IRS doesn't evaluate each of Marcus's side activities in isolation for this purpose. **The $400 threshold applies to combined net self-employment earnings across all activities, not to each one separately.**

Marcus's total: $4,200 + $3,100 + $320 = **$7,620** in combined net self-employment income — a number that was already well over the $400 threshold before the eBay income was even added, because his tutoring and photography income alone already cross it.

## Why This Matters Even Though the Threshold Was Already Crossed

Here's the detail that's easy to miss: since Marcus's combined self-employment income is already taxable regardless of the eBay portion, the eBay $320 isn't sitting in some separate "too small to matter" category — it's part of the same combined total that his self-employment tax is calculated on. Every dollar of it is subject to the same 15.3% self-employment tax as his tutoring and photography income, exactly as if it had come from either of his other two activities.

**Self-employment tax on the full $7,620 combined: roughly $1,077** — and the $320 eBay portion contributes its proportional share to that total (roughly $45 of it), not zero, and not some reduced rate because it happened to be the smallest of his three income sources.

## How Marcus's Filing Actually Works

Because his three activities are genuinely different lines of work — tutoring, photography, and reselling — standard guidance points toward **separate Schedule C forms** for each, keeping the income and expense tracking clean per activity. But all three combine into **one Schedule SE** for the self-employment tax calculation, which is where his $7,620 combined total (not three separate calculations) determines what he owes.

## Why Marcus's Instinct Almost Cost Him Accuracy, Not Money

To be clear about what's actually at stake here: Marcus wasn't at risk of owing less tax by treating the eBay income casually — his tutoring and photography income already established that he owes self-employment tax this year regardless. The real risk was **incomplete or inaccurate reporting** — if he'd mentally filed the eBay sales under "too small to bother tracking," he'd risk underreporting his actual combined income, which creates a mismatch if any of these income sources get reported to the IRS independently (through 1099 forms or payment platform reporting) even at these modest amounts.

## The Practical Lesson for Anyone With More Than One Side Activity

Once you have any self-employment activity that clearly crosses the $400 threshold on its own, every other piece of self-employment income you have — no matter how small — becomes part of the same combined calculation. There's no separate "too small to count" carve-out for additional income sources once the combined total is already taxable. Track everything, even the small stuff, because it's already part of the number that matters.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**If Marcus's eBay sales were personal items sold at a loss, would they still count?** No — selling personal items for less than their original purchase price (a used camera lens sold below what you paid, for example) generally isn't taxable income at all, regardless of the self-employment threshold discussion. This case assumes Marcus's eBay activity involves items resold at a profit as part of an ongoing, business-like activity, not occasional personal item sales at a loss — the distinction matters and is worth applying accurately to your own situation.

**Does Marcus need to make quarterly estimated payments for three separate side activities?** No — quarterly estimated payments are based on his total expected tax liability across all combined income sources (his teaching salary's withholding plus his combined self-employment income), calculated once per quarter, not separately per gig. The complexity is in tracking and reporting three activities accurately on his return, not in making three sets of estimated payments throughout the year.
